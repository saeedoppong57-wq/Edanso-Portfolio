About
Dr. Edward Kwame Danso

As a health professional, I have numerous conversations with patients about Western medicine. Many believe it holds all the answers — when in fact it does not. When a doctor says there is no cure, it does not mean there is none. It simply means medical science has not yet found a solution for that condition.
In my view, it is a profound mistake when any medical professional steals hope from a patient. Embedding the idea that there is no cure helps keep the patient sick. Hope is medicine.

“Time, sympathy, and understanding must be lavishly dispensed, but the reward is to be found in that personal bond which forms the greatest satisfaction of the practice of medicine. One of the essential qualities of the clinician is interest in humanity, for the secret of the care of the patient is in caring for the patient.”
— Dr. Francis W. Peabody

I am working for Jesus to heal the sick and to promote good health and well-being for the ageing population globally — to prevent and treat diseases prone to them. With over 29 years of professional experience and 14 years researching regenerative medicine (Geriatrics), I have changed the livelihood of several people and deployed numerous innovative solutions.

Mission

We Help Too Late — Every Time

Medicine, Hope & Humanity

Many believe Western medicine has all the answers when in fact it does not. As someone who helps people recover from chronic health conditions, I explain to my clients that when a doctor tells them there is no cure, it simply means medical science has not found a solution yet.

I believe most of our medical and pharmaceutical community deserves our respect. And I believe that integrating regenerative approaches with compassionate care can unlock healing that conventional medicine has not yet reached.

An advocate for change — I love to help people, and to educate tomorrow’s healthcare providers for the needs of the ageing population. We should not wait for their death to remind us of their worth.

We write great tributes, show great concern, make huge donations, express deep sympathy — when those who needed it are gone. Where was all that when it mattered most?
